A spreadsheet (CSV) with latitude and longitude columns, or a GeoJSON, GPX, or KML file. Your points and shapes are added to your drawings so you can style, edit, and save them.

Search free public layers — try “rivers”, “earthquakes”, “national parks”, or “population”. ★ Featured layers show up first.

Turn layers on and off, reorder them, or remove ones you don’t need.

Measure distance along a line or the area of a shape. Click on the map to place points; double-click to finish.

Pick a color, then draw points, lines, and shapes with the tools below — or add a text label. To recolor something, select it, then pick a new color.

Click anywhere on the map to drop a pin. The latitude and longitude lock in place until you click somewhere else. You can also type coordinates below to jump there.

Click the map to drop a pin and see the place name, address, elevation, and current weather. The exact latitude/longitude show above.

A quick tour of every tool. GIS stands for Geographic Information System — software for making and studying maps.

🗺️Basemap — change the background map (satellite, streets, topographic…).
➕Add data — find free public map layers and add them on top.
📋Layers — show, hide, reorder, or remove your layers.
📏Measure — find how long or how big something is.
✏️Sketch — draw your own points, lines, and shapes.
📍Place — click the map to drop a pin and see its place name, latitude/longitude, and elevation.
🔍Search (top of map) — find a place or address.
💾Save / Open — save your map as a file, then open it again later. The file follows the ArcGIS web map format, so it also works in ArcGIS Online.
